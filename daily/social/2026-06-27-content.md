## C) TWITTER/X THREAD (7 tweets)

High notes shouldn't feel like you're being punched in the throat.

Every singer I work with who "can't hit high notes" has the same problem — not lack of range, but lack of coordination. Here's the 3-step fix that works in minutes, not months:

**Tweet 2:**
Mistake #1: Reaching UP with your chin.

When you crane your neck upward for high notes, you pull your larynx up and close your throat space. You're literally strangling the note before it comes out. The note fights back. You feel it as strain.

**Tweet 3:**
Mistake #2: Pushing MORE air.

High notes need faster air, not more air. Think of a balloon — if you squeeze it hard, it screams and pops. If you let it release steadily, it glides. Your voice works the same way. Less pressure = more freedom.

**Tweet 4:**
The fix starts with your soft palate (the roof of your mouth).

Before you sing a high note, think "beginning of a yawn." Feel the back of your mouth lift and open? That space is your high note room. The note resonates UP into that space, not FORCED up from your throat.

**Tweet 5:**
Try the Ha → Ho drill:

Sing a comfortable note on "ha" (open, throaty). Same note on "ho" (rounded, lighter). The "ho" shape automatically shifts your resonance forward and up — out of your throat, into your face. That buzz in your lips? That's head voice.

**Tweet 6:**
The rule I teach every student:

"If a high note feels harder than a low note, something is wrong with your coordination." High notes should feel LIGHTER — like the air is doing the work, not your throat. If you're straining, stop. Reset. Change the shape, not the force.
