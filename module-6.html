Lesson 6.1: Why Articulation Matters

You can have perfect pitch, beautiful tone, and stunning dynamics — but if the audience can't understand the words, the message is lost.

Articulation is the clarity of your pronunciation while singing. It's the difference between "singing notes" and "communicating words."

The 3 Pillars of Vocal Articulation

  • Vowel clarity: Pure, consistent vowels that carry across your range
  • Consonant precision: Clear consonants that define words without interrupting the tone
  • Word connection: Smooth transitions between words that maintain the legato line

The NYVC principle: In singing, vowels carry the emotion. Consonants carry the meaning. You need both. A singer with beautiful vowels but muddy consonants sounds like they're singing in a language no one understands. A singer with sharp consonants but weak vowels sounds like they're speaking, not singing.

Common Articulation Problems

  • Swallowing endings: "Love" becomes "luh," "grace" becomes "gray." The final consonant disappears.
  • Over-articulating: Every consonant is punched. Sounds choppy and unnatural.
  • Vowel distortion: Singing "ee" with too much jaw tension, making it sound strained.
  • Language mixing: Pronouncing words from one language with the accent of another unintentionally.

Lesson 6.2: The International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA) — Vowels

The IPA is a system where each symbol represents exactly one sound, regardless of language. For singers, it's the difference between guessing and knowing exactly what vowel shape to make.

The 5 Pure Vowels

These are the foundation. Every other vowel is a variation of these:

IPAEnglish ExampleMouth ShapeTongue Position
[i]"ee" in "see"Lips spread, jaw nearly closedHigh and forward
[e]"ay" in "day" (pure)Lips slightly spreadMid-high and forward
[a]"ah" in "father"Mouth open, jaw relaxedLow and flat
[o]"o" in "go" (pure)Lips roundedMid-high and back
[u]"oo" in "food"Lips tightly roundedHigh and back

The Vowel Ladder

These vowels form a ladder from most closed [i] to most open [a] and back to most closed [u]:

[i] → [e] → [a] → [o] → [u]

When you sing up in pitch, vowels naturally move toward [i] and [u] (more closed). When you sing down, they move toward [a] (more open). This is vowel modification — and IPA gives you precise control over it.

Exercise: Sing the 5 pure vowels on one comfortable pitch: [i] [e] [a] [o] [u]. Hold each for 4 seconds. Notice how your mouth shape changes. Now do it ascending in pitch — notice how the vowels naturally get smaller/tighter as you go up.

Lesson 6.3: IPA — Consonants and Consonant Clusters

Vowels carry the tone. Consonants carry the words. In singing, consonants need to be clear but not disruptive — they should define the words without breaking the legato line.

Consonant Categories for Singers

TypeSoundsSinging Tip
Plosives[b], [p], [d], [t], [g], [k]Don't over-punch. A gentle touch is enough.
Fricatives[v], [f], [ð], [θ], [z], [s], [ʃ], [ʒ]Maintain steady air. Don't let them overpower the vowel.
Nasals[m], [n], [ŋ]These actually help resonance. Let them buzz.
Approximants[l], [ɹ], [j], [w]Keep them smooth. They connect vowels to consonants.
Affricates[tʃ] "ch", [dʒ] "j"Give them a clean release. Don't rush.

Consonant Clusters

When consonants appear together ("str" in "strong," "sts" in "tests"), singers often either:

  • Drop consonants (srong, tess)
  • Over-articulate (STRO-ONG, TEST-S)

The NYVC rule: In clusters, keep the first consonant clear and the last consonant clear. The middle ones can be softened. "Strong" → clear "s," clear "ng," soft "tr" in between.

Lesson 6.4: Singing in English — Standard Pronunciation

English is the most common language in Nigerian worship and pop music. But "English" pronunciation varies wildly. For singing, we use a standard, clear English that's understood across regions.

Key English Vowel Sounds for Singers

IPAExample WordsCommon Mistake
[iː]see, free, keySaying "ih" [ɪ] instead of "ee" [iː]
[ɪ]sit, in, itMaking it too tense, like [iː]
[æ]cat, man, praiseNarrowing it to "eh" [ɛ]
[ɑː]father, heart, GodRounding it to "aw" [ɔː]
[ə]the, a, about (unstressed)Over-pronouncing unstressed syllables

The Schwa [ə] — The Singer's Best Friend

The schwa is the most common vowel in English. It's the neutral "uh" sound in unstressed syllables: "the" [ðə], "a" [ə], "about" [əˈbaʊt].

Why it matters: Singers often over-pronounce unstressed syllables, which makes English sound unnatural. "The" should be [ðə], not [ði]. "And" should be [ənd], not [ænd]. The schwa keeps the rhythm flowing.

NYVC rule: Stressed syllables get full vowels. Unstressed syllables get schwa [ə]. This is what makes English singing sound natural, not forced.

Lesson 6.5: Singing in Nigerian Languages

Nigeria has 500+ languages. Each has its own vowel and consonant system. Here's how to approach singing in the major ones:

Yoruba

  • Vowels: 7 pure vowels — [i], [e], [ɛ], [a], [ɔ], [o], [u]. More than English!
  • Tone: Yoruba is tonal — the same syllable with different tones means different things. Singing must respect the tone patterns of the language.
  • Key tip: The open vowels [ɛ] and [ɔ] are crucial. Don't collapse them into [e] and [o].

Igbo

  • Vowels: 8 vowels — includes [ɪ], [ʊ], [ɛ], [ɔ] in addition to the standard 5.
  • Consonants: Unique sounds like [gb] (implosive), [kp] (ejective). These are produced differently from English consonants.
  • Tone: Also tonal. The melody of the song must align with the tone of the words.

Hausa

  • Vowels: 5 basic vowels, but with short and long distinctions that affect meaning.
  • Consonants: Unique sounds like [ɓ] (implosive b), [ɗ] (implosive d), [ƙ] (ejective k).
  • Key tip: Hausa has emphatic consonants that require more breath pressure. Don't soften them.

Universal Rule for Nigerian Languages

Respect the tone. In tonal languages, the melody of your song MUST align with the natural tone of the words. If you sing a high tone on a low-tone syllable, you change the meaning. When in doubt, ask a native speaker to check your pronunciation.

Lesson 6.6: Applying IPA to Song Lyrics — Practical Transcription

Now let's put it all together. Here's how to transcribe any song's lyrics into IPA for precise pronunciation.

The Transcription Process

  1. Write out the lyrics — one line at a time
  2. Identify each vowel sound — use the IPA chart
  3. Identify each consonant sound — especially clusters
  4. Mark stress patterns — which syllables are emphasized?
  5. Sing from the IPA — not from the English spelling

Example: "Amazing Grace" (First Line)

WordIPANotes
Amazing[əˈmeɪzɪŋ]Schwa on first syllable, "z" not "s"
Grace[ɡɹeɪs]"r" is approximant [ɹ], not rolled
how[haʊ]Diphthong — starts at [a], ends at [ʊ]
sweet[swiːt]Long [iː], clear [t] at the end
the[ðə]Schwa [ə], not "thee" [ði]
sound[saʊnd]Diphthong [aʊ], clear [nd] cluster

Why this matters: When you sing "the" as [ðə] instead of [ði], it sounds more natural and keeps the rhythm flowing. When you sing "Amazing" as [əˈmeɪzɪŋ] instead of [ɑːˈmɑːzɪŋ], it sounds like standard English, not forced. IPA removes the guesswork.
