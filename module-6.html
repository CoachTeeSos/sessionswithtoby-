Lesson 6.1: Why Articulation Matters

Articulation is how clearly you form words while singing. Poor articulation means the audience hears melody but can't understand the message. Great articulation means every word lands.

The Articulators

Five parts of your mouth shape words: lips, tongue, teeth, hard palate, and soft palate. When any of these are lazy, your words become muddy.

The NYVC principle: Singing is sustained speech. If you wouldn't mumble it in conversation, don't mumble it when you sing. Every consonant needs intention.

Lesson 6.2: The IPA Vowel System

The International Phonetic Alphabet gives us precise names for vowel shapes. This removes ambiguity — when a coach says "sing [i]," every singer produces the exact same vowel.

Lesson 6.3: Consonants — The Backbone of Clarity

Vowels carry the tone. Consonants carry the meaning. Without clear consonants, lyrics become mush.

The Problem Consonants for Singers

  • S and Z: Can become harsh and sibilant. Solution: keep the "s" short and controlled
  • T and D: Often dropped at the end of words. Solution: exaggerate the tongue tip contact
  • R: Can distort the vowel. Solution: soften it, especially in British-influenced singing
  • L: Can become dark and swallow the vowel. Solution: keep the tongue tip up
  • M, N, NG: These are your friends — they create nasal resonance and help placement

NYVC exercise: Exaggerate every consonant in a phrase like "The love of God is broader than the measure of man's mind." Overdo it. Then pull back 20%. That's your performance clarity.

Lesson 6.4: Singing in English — Diction Rules

English has specific challenges for singers: silent letters, schwa sounds, and the tendency to swallow endings.

The Schwa [ə]

The most common vowel sound in English — the "uh" in "the," "a," "of." In singing, schwas should be brief and light. Don't give them full vowel weight.

Common English Diction Mistakes

  • Singing "the" as "thee" instead of "thuh" before consonants
  • Dropping the final "d" in "and," "God," "Lord"
  • Over-pronouncing "r" in words like "father," "water"
  • Singing "ing" as "ing" instead of "in'" in casual contexts

Lesson 6.5: Singing in Nigerian Languages

Yoruba, Igbo, and Hausa each have unique phonetic characteristics. Understanding them helps you sing in these languages with authenticity.

Yoruba

  • Tonal language — pitch changes meaning
  • Vowels are pure and consistent: a, e, ẹ, i, o, ọ, u
  • Consonants are generally softer than English
  • The "gb" and "kp" sounds are unique — practice them slowly

Igbo

  • Also tonal — melody must respect the tonal patterns
  • Rich vowel system with nasalized vowels
  • Consonant clusters like "gb," "gh," "nw," "ny" need careful articulation

Hausa

  • Tonal with two basic tones (high and low) plus a falling tone
  • Glottal stops (represented by ') are important — don't skip them
  • Vowels can be short or long — length changes meaning

Universal principle: Whatever language you sing in, the vowels carry the tone and the consonants carry the meaning. Master both.

Lesson 6.6: Putting It All Together — Clear Singing in Any Language

The goal isn't perfection — it's intelligibility. Can the audience understand you? If yes, your articulation is working.

The Clarity Checklist

  • Are your vowels pure and consistent?
  • Are your consonants clear — especially at the beginnings and ends of words?
  • Are you respecting the phonetic rules of the language you're singing in?
  • Can someone who doesn't know the song still understand the words?
