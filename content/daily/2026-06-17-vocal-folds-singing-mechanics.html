How Your Voice Works: Vocal Folds,
Breath, and Sound Explained

You've used your voice every day for years. But most singers have no idea what's actually happening inside their throat. Here's the mechanics — and a 90-second exercise to feel every part of it.

Your Voice Is a Wind Instrument (Sort Of)

Here's something that surprises most people: your voice isn't a string instrument. It's closer to a brass instrument — like a trumpet or trombone. Your vocal folds vibrate to create sound, and your throat, mouth, and nasal passages shape that sound into vowels, consonants, and singing tones.

Understanding how this system works isn't academic. It's practical. When you know what's happening inside your throat, you stop guessing and start coordinating. You stop pushing and start adjusting.

So let's look at the three systems that create your voice — the same way every vocal pedagogy method (Estill, CVT, NYVC) breaks it down.

The Three Systems of Your Voice

01
The Power Source — Breath. Your diaphragm pushes air upward from your lungs. This airstream travels up your windpipe and reaches your vocal folds. The speed, pressure, and consistency of this airflow determine your volume, pitch stability, and how long you can hold a phrase. When breath support drops, everything else collapses. Learn the breath support coordination here.
02
The Vibrator — Your Vocal Folds. Your vocal folds (sometimes called vocal cords) are two small, muscular folds of tissue inside your larynx (voice box). When air passes between them, they vibrate — opening and closing rapidly, hundreds of times per second. The speed of vibration determines your pitch: faster vibration = higher pitch, slower vibration = lower pitch. Tightening the folds raises pitch; relaxing them lowers it.
03
The Resonator — Your Vocal Tract. Your throat, mouth, tongue, soft palate, and nasal passages form your vocal tract — the resonating chamber that shapes raw vibration into recognizable sound. The shape of this chamber determines your tone, your vowel sounds, and the unique color of your voice. This is why singers can sound so different from each other even on the same note — they're shaping their vocal tract differently.

Every singing problem traces back to one of these three systems. Strain? Usually the vibrator (vocal folds) is being forced. Running out of air? Power source (breath) isn't coordinated. Tinny or thin tone? The resonator (vocal tract) isn't shaped correctly.

What Most Singers Get Wrong About Pitch

Here's where it gets interesting. Most singers think pitch is about their throat — "reach higher," "aim for the note." But pitch is actually determined by vocal fold tension, and that tension is controlled by muscles inside your larynx that you can't directly feel.

You can't "think" your way to a high note. You have to coordinate the relationship between breath pressure and fold tension. Too much breath with too little tension = breathy, weak sound. Too much tension with too little air = strain and cracking.

This is the coordination that professional singers train — and the reason why simple advice like "just relax" or "push harder" never works. You need to train the relationship between systems, not just one at a time.

The Hands-On Voice Discovery (90-Second Exercise)

This exercise lets you physically feel all three systems working together. Do it standing up, relaxed.

1
Feel your larynx (voice box). Place two fingers on the front of your throat, just above your collarbone. You'll feel a small, hard shape — that's your larynx. Hold your fingers there. This is where your vocal folds live.
2
Hum a comfortable low note. Close your lips gently and hum on an "mmm" sound — something low and easy, like the bottom of your speaking range. You should feel a buzz or vibration under your fingertips. That's your vocal folds vibrating. If you feel nothing, hum louder or lower until you do.
3
Slide up slowly to a high note. Without opening your mouth, slide the pitch upward like a siren — low to high, in one smooth motion. Feel the vibration under your fingers change. At the bottom, it's a loose, massy buzz. At the top, it becomes thinner, tighter, more focused. That's your vocal folds stretching and tightening to change pitch.
4
Feel your breath support kick in. As you slide higher, notice that your body naturally wants to push more air — your belly tightens, your chest lifts. That's your power source responding to the increased tension in the vibrator. Let it happen naturally. Don't force it, don't hold back.
5
Add resonance — open your mouth. On the same pitch (hold a comfortable mid-range note), transition from "mmm" to "ahh" — open your mouth wide while keeping the same pitch. You'll hear the tone get louder and richer. That's your vocal tract (resonator) opening up and amplifying the sound your folds are already making.
6
6
Repeat 5 times. Go through the full sequence — hum, slide, feel the larynx, open to "ahh" — five times. Each round, pay attention to which system you're feeling. Power source: belly and diaphragm. Vibrator: buzz under your fingers. Resonator: tone quality in your mouth.
~90 seconds total · Do this before every practice session · 5 rounds minimum

Why this exercise matters: Most singers operate on feel alone — and their feelings lie to them. They think they're supporting when they're actually pushing. They think they're relaxed when they're actually tension-free because the note is low. Feeling the physical systems with your hands gives you an objective reference point. Your fingers don't lie.

Why Knowing This Changes Everything

When a singer comes to me with a "high note problem," the solution is almost never "try harder." It's almost always a coordination issue between the power source and the vibrator. The vocal folds need more tension (for the higher pitch) but also more breath pressure (to keep vibrating at that tension). If either system fails, the note cracks, goes breathy, or doesn't come out at all.

Understanding this changes how you practice. Instead of repeating a phrase 20 times hoping it gets better, you can diagnose which system is failing and fix that specific system. Is the note breathy? Your vocal folds aren't closing enough — work on fold adduction. Is it strained? Too much tension, not enough breath — reduce the throat squeeze and support more from the diaphragm. Is it thin and weak? Your resonator needs more space — drop your jaw, lift your soft palate.

This is the difference between practicing and training. Practice repeats. Training diagnoses and adjusts.

Note: This article is for educational purposes. If you experience persistent hoarseness, pain while singing, or voice loss lasting more than two weeks, please consult an ENT specialist or laryngologist. Vocal health issues require medical evaluation.

The Singer Who Couldn't Hit High Notes (And What Fixed It)

A common scenario: a singer who can belt low notes beautifully but loses everything above a certain pitch. The voice cracks, goes airy, or just stops. They've been told to "open their throat" and "support from the diaphragm" — but it hasn't helped.

Here's what's usually happening: their vocal folds are fine. Their breath support is adequate. The problem is the transition point — the pitch where their voice needs to shift from "chest voice" (thick fold vibration) to "head voice" (thin fold vibration). At that transition, the coordination between breath pressure and fold tension has to change. If it doesn't, the voice breaks.

The fix isn't a magic exercise. It's training the transition — slowly, deliberately, with feedback. Sliding exercises (like the one above) that pass through the transition zone repeatedly, at low volume, until the coordination becomes automatic. Most singers skip this because it feels too simple. But simple doesn't mean easy. It means precise.
