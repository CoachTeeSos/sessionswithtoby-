How to Fix a Breathy Voice:
The 2-Minute Closure Exercise

You sing and hear air in your tone — a whisper layered under every note. It's not a breath support problem. It's a vocal fold problem. Here's the fix that works in under 2 minutes.

Breathy Voice: What's Actually Happening

Here's the thing most singers get wrong about breathiness: they think they're using too much air. So they try to "use less breath" — and the problem gets worse.

The real issue isn't how much air you're using. It's that your vocal folds aren't closing fully when they vibrate. Air escapes through the gap between the folds before it can be converted into sound. That escaping air is what you hear as breathiness — a hiss or whisper underneath your tone.

Think of it like a door that doesn't shut all the way. It doesn't matter how little wind is blowing — if the gap is there, air gets through. The fix isn't reducing the wind. It's closing the door.

In vocal pedagogy, this is called vocal fold adduction — the ability of your vocal folds to come together cleanly and completely during phonation. Some people have naturally weaker adduction. Others develop it from habit — singing too softly for too long, avoiding loud volumes, or imitating breathy singers they admire.

The good news: adduction is trainable. It's a muscular coordination, and like any coordination, it responds to targeted exercise.

3 Reasons Your Voice Is Breathy

01
Habitual under-singing. Many singers — especially those who started singing quietly in their rooms — develop a pattern of singing with incomplete fold closure. It feels safe and intimate. But over months and years, the muscles that close the folds get weaker from disuse. The breathy pattern becomes your default tone, and clean tone starts to feel "too loud" or "harsh" by comparison.
02
Imitating breathy artists. Plenty of popular singers use breathiness as a stylistic choice. If you've spent years trying to sound like them, you may have trained your vocal folds to stay slightly apart. This is common and fixable — but it requires retraining your ear as much as your voice. Clean tone needs to start sounding "right" to you again.
03
Weak thyroarytenoid engagement. The muscles inside your larynx that bring your vocal folds together are called the thyroarytenoid muscles (sometimes shortened to "TA muscles"). If these muscles aren't engaging fully — often because the cricothyroid muscles, which stretch the folds for higher pitches, are dominating — the folds can't close properly. This is especially common in the upper range, where many singers go breathy on high notes. Related: why your voice cracks on high notes.

The 2-Minute Vocal Fold Closure Exercise

This exercise uses a specific sound pattern that forces your vocal folds to close more completely. It's based on the "glottal onsets" and "coup de glotte" techniques used in classical and contemporary vocal training.

Important: This exercise should feel easy. If you feel strain, tension, or pain, stop and reduce your volume. You're coordinating muscles, not forcing them.

The "Gug" Closure Drill (2 Minutes)

Do this standing up, relaxed. Keep your jaw loose and your tongue resting behind your bottom teeth.

1
Start with a silent "G." Close your mouth, build a tiny bit of air pressure behind your soft palate (like you're about to say "go"), and release it as a firm "guh" sound. The "G" requires your vocal folds to close briefly before the sound starts. Feel that small "catch" or "click" at the beginning — that's your folds adducting. Do this 3 times, just the "guh" sound, medium volume.
2
Add a vowel: "Gug." Now extend the sound: "guh-uh" — the "guh" into an "uh" vowel (like the "u" in "gut"). The transition from "guh" to "uh" should be clean — no air leak between them. If you hear a "h" sound between the consonant and vowel ("guh-huh"), your folds are opening too early. Keep it tight: "guh-uh" as one connected sound.
3
Repeat on a single note. Pick a comfortable mid-range note (for most singers, this is around A3 to D4). Sing "gug" on that note — one "gug" per breath. Focus on the attack: the moment the sound starts should be clean, not breathy. You should feel a small "buzz" at the start of each note. Do 5 repetitions.
4
Extend the vowel. Now sing "guh-uuuuh" — hold the "uh" vowel for 4 beats while keeping the tone clean. The challenge is maintaining the same clean closure from the "guh" through the entire vowel. If the tone gets breathy in the middle, your folds are drifting apart. Reset with another "gug" and try again. Do 5 repetitions.
5
Test it on a scale. Sing a 5-note ascending scale (do-re-mi-fa-sol) using "gug" on each note. As you go higher, the tendency is to go breathy. Fight it. Keep every note as clean as the first one. If you lose closure on a particular note, go back to that note in isolation and repeat it 3 times before continuing.
6
Transfer to a phrase. Pick a line from a song you know well. Sing it once the way you normally would — notice where the breathiness creeps in. Now sing it again, imagining each note starts with a tiny "gug" — not actually singing the "G," but using that same clean onset feeling. The tone should be noticeably clearer.
~2 minutes total · Do this daily for 2 weeks · Record yourself on Day 1 and Day 14 to hear the difference

What you should notice: After 2 minutes of this drill, your speaking voice will probably sound slightly different — clearer, more present. That's a good sign. It means your vocal folds are learning to close more efficiently. This carries over into singing within a few days of consistent practice.

Common Mistakes (And How to Avoid Them)

✦
Confusing "clean" with "loud." A clean tone doesn't have to be loud. You can sing softly with full fold closure — it just requires more coordination. If you're pushing volume to force closure, you're building tension, not coordination. Start at medium volume and work softer as the coordination improves.
✦
Overdoing the glottal onset. The "guh" in this exercise should be firm but not aggressive. If you're slamming your vocal folds together hard enough to feel a "thunk" in your throat, you're overdoing it. The closure should feel like a firm handshake, not a slam. Excessive glottal onsets can cause vocal fatigue if done too forcefully.
✦
Expecting instant results on high notes. Closure is harder at higher pitches because the vocal folds are stretched thinner. Don't expect the same clarity on your highest notes right away. Build the coordination in your mid-range first, then gradually extend upward over days and weeks. Breath support also plays a role — learn the coordination here.
Note: This article is for educational purposes. If you experience persistent hoarseness, pain while singing, or voice loss lasting more than two weeks, please consult an ENT specialist or laryngologist. Some causes of breathiness (such as vocal nodules or polyps) require medical evaluation and cannot be fixed with exercises alone.

Why "Just Sing Louder" Doesn't Work

Here's what I hear from a lot of singers: "My coach told me to sing louder to fix the breathiness, but it just got louder AND breathy."

That's because volume alone doesn't fix incomplete fold closure. If your folds aren't closing, more air just means more air escaping — louder breathiness. The coordination has to come first. Once your folds are closing cleanly, you can add volume (more breath pressure) and the tone will get louder without getting breathier. The order matters: closure first, then power.

This is one of those things that's easy to understand but takes consistent practice to rewire. Most singers see noticeable improvement within 1-2 weeks of daily practice. Full integration — where clean tone becomes your new default — usually takes 4-6 weeks.
