Lesson 3.1: Pitch, Scales, and Keys

Pitch is how high or low a note is. Scales are organized patterns of pitches. Keys tell you which scale a song uses. These aren't abstract concepts — they're tools that help you learn songs faster and sing in tune.

The Chromatic Scale

All 12 notes in Western music, in order: C, C#, D, D#, E, F, F#, G, G#, A, A#, B, then back to C. On a piano, this is every key — white and black.

Why singers care: When you know the key of a song, you know which notes are "home" (the root) and which create tension (the 7th, the 4th). This helps you anticipate where the melody is going and sing with more confidence.

Minor Scale — The Emotional Scale

The natural minor scale pattern: W-H-W-W-H-W-W. Starting on A: A → B → C → D → E → F → G → A

Minor scales sound sad, serious, or intense. Most Afrobeats and gospel use a mix of major and minor — the minor gives emotional depth.

Lesson 3.2: Rhythm and Time Signatures

Rhythm is the pattern of sounds in time. As a singer, your rhythm determines whether you sound tight or sloppy. You can have perfect pitch and still sound bad with poor rhythm.

Beat and Pulse

The beat is the steady pulse you tap your foot to. Every song has one. When you clap along to a song, you're clapping the beat.

Time Signatures

The time signature tells you how many beats are in each bar and what note gets one beat:

  • 4/4 (Common Time): 4 beats per bar. Most pop, gospel, Afrobeats, rock. Count: 1-2-3-4, 1-2-3-4
  • 3/4 (Waltz Time): 3 beats per bar. Some ballads, hymns. Count: 1-2-3, 1-2-3
  • 6/8: 6 beats per bar, felt as 2 groups of 3. Many African rhythms, some gospel

Exercise: Play a song you know well. Tap your foot to the beat. Now count "1-2-3-4" with your taps. Does it feel natural? If you're counting "1-2-3" and it feels right, it's probably in 3/4 or 6/8.

Syncopation — The Secret of Groove

Syncopation is when you accent the off-beats — the "and" between the main beats. Instead of 1-2-3-4, you emphasize 1-and-2-and-3-and-4-and.

This is what makes Afrobeats and funk feel so alive. The voice and instruments play around the beat, not just on it.

For Nigerian singers: Highlife, Fuji, and Afrobeats all use heavy syncopation. If you only sing on the main beat, you'll sound stiff. Practice singing slightly behind the beat — it creates that relaxed, groovy feel.

Task 3.2

Choose a song. Tap the beat while listening. Now try clapping on the off-beats (the "and" between each beat). Record yourself in BandLab: first sing the melody on the beat, then try it with syncopation. Compare the two.

Lesson 3.3: Intervals — The Building Blocks of Melody

An interval is the distance between two notes. Every melody is just a series of intervals. When you can recognize intervals by ear, you can learn songs faster and sing more accurately.

Why singers care: When you hear a melody, your brain processes the intervals. If you can consciously identify them, you can reproduce them faster. This is the foundation of ear training.

Interval Singing Exercise

Sing these intervals from a comfortable starting note:

  1. Start on any note, go up a Major 3rd (4 semitones) — sounds like "Happy Birthday" opening
  2. Start on any note, go up a Perfect 5th (7 semitones) — sounds like "Twinkle Twinkle"
  3. Start on any note, go up an Octave (12 semitones) — same note, higher
  4. Now go DOWN a Minor 3rd (3 semitones) — sounds like "Greensleeves"

Lesson 3.4: Chord Progressions and Harmony Basics

A chord is three or more notes played together. A chord progression is a sequence of chords. Most songs are built on a repeating chord progression.

The I-IV-V-I Progression

The most common progression in Western music. In the key of C major:

  • I = C major (C-E-G) — "home," feels resolved
  • IV = F major (F-A-C) — "moving," feels like a journey
  • V = G major (G-B-D) — "tension," wants to go home
  • I = C major — back home, feels complete

This progression is in thousands of songs: "Let It Be," "Lean on Me," "No Woman No Cry," countless gospel songs.

The vi-IV-I-V Progression

The "pop progression" — used in most modern pop, Afrobeats, and worship songs. In C major:

  • vi = A minor — emotional, introspective
  • IV = F major — building
  • I = C major — resolution
  • V = G major — tension, sets up the repeat

NYVC insight: When you know the chord progression, you can predict where the melody is going. Singers who understand harmony don't just sing notes — they sing with the chords. They emphasize chord tones (notes that belong to the current chord) for a grounded, in-tune sound.

Task 3.4

Find a simple song (gospel hymns work great). Identify the key and try to figure out the chord progression using I, IV, and V. You can use a piano app or YouTube tutorials to help. Write down the progression (e.g., I-IV-I-V-I).

Lesson 3.5: Song Structure — Verse, Chorus, Bridge

Most songs follow a predictable structure. Understanding it helps you learn songs faster, know when to sing with more or less intensity, and even write your own songs.

Common Song Structures

  • Verse-Chorus-Verse-Chorus-Bridge-Chorus — Most pop, Afrobeats, worship songs
  • Verse-Verse-Chorus-Verse-Chorus — Simpler songs, some hymns
  • AABA — Classic 32-bar form, jazz standards, older hymns
  • Intro-Verse-Chorus-Verse-Chorus-Bridge-Chorus-Outro — Full pop structure

What Each Section Does

  • Verse: Tells the story, builds the narrative. Usually lower intensity, more conversational.
  • Pre-Chorus: Builds tension toward the chorus. "The climb before the peak."
  • Chorus: The main message, the hook. Highest energy, most memorable. This is what people sing along to.
  • Bridge: The contrast. Different melody, different chords, often the emotional peak. Provides variety before the final chorus.
  • Outro: The wind-down. Can be a repeat of the chorus, a fade, or a final statement.

Performance insight: Your dynamics should follow the structure. Verse = softer, more intimate. Chorus = fuller, more power. Bridge = emotional peak. Don't sing everything at the same volume — that's the difference between a flat performance and a captivating one.
