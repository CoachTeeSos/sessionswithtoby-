Lesson 3.1: Pitch, Scales, and Keys

Pitch is how high or low a note is. Scales are organized patterns of pitches. Keys tell you which scale a song uses. These aren't abstract concepts — they're tools that help you learn songs faster and sing in tune.

The Chromatic Scale

All 12 notes in Western music: C, C#, D, D#, E, F, F#, G, G#, A, A#, B, then back to C. On a piano, this is every key — white and black.

Major Scale — The Foundation

The most common scale in Western music. The pattern from any note is: Whole-Whole-Half-Whole-Whole-Whole-Half (W-W-H-W-W-W-H). Starting on C: C → D → E → F → G → A → B → C.

Why singers care: When you know the key of a song, you know which notes are "home" (the root) and which create tension (the 7th, the 4th). This helps you anticipate where the melody is going and sing with more confidence.

Minor Scale — The Emotional Scale

The natural minor scale pattern: W-H-W-W-H-W-W. Starting on A: A → B → C → D → E → F → G → A. Minor scales sound sad, serious, or intense. Most Afrobeats and gospel use a mix of major and minor.

Lesson 3.2: Rhythm and Time Signatures

Rhythm is the pattern of sounds in time. As a singer, your rhythm determines whether you sound tight or sloppy. You can have perfect pitch and still sound bad with poor rhythm.

Beat and Pulse

The beat is the steady pulse you tap your foot to. Every song has one. When you clap along to a song, you're clapping the beat.

Time Signatures

  • 4/4 (Common Time): 4 beats per bar. Most pop, gospel, Afrobeats, rock. Count: 1-2-3-4, 1-2-3-4
  • 3/4 (Waltz Time): 3 beats per bar. Some ballads, hymns. Count: 1-2-3, 1-2-3
  • 6/8: 6 beats per bar, felt as 2 groups of 3. Many African rhythms, some gospel

Syncopation — The Secret of Groove

Syncopation is when you accent the off-beats — the "and" between the main beats. This is what makes Afrobeats and funk feel so alive. The voice and instruments play around the beat, not just on it.

For Nigerian singers: Highlife, Fuji, and Afrobeats all use heavy syncopation. If you only sing on the main beat, you'll sound stiff. Practice singing slightly behind the beat — it creates that relaxed, groovy feel.

Lesson 3.3: Intervals — The Building Blocks of Melody

An interval is the distance between two notes. Every melody is just a series of intervals. When you can recognize intervals by ear, you can learn songs faster and sing more accurately.

Common Intervals (from C)

  • Major 3rd (4 semitones): C → E. Sounds like "Happy Birthday" opening
  • Perfect 5th (7 semitones): C → G. Sounds like "Twinkle Twinkle" start
  • Octave (12 semitones): C → C. Same note, higher
  • Minor 3rd (3 semitones): C → Eb. Sounds sad, melancholic

Why singers care: When you hear a melody, your brain processes the intervals. If you can consciously identify them, you can reproduce them faster. This is the foundation of ear training.

Lesson 3.4: Chord Progressions and Harmony Basics

A chord is three or more notes played together. A chord progression is a sequence of chords. Most songs are built on a repeating chord progression.

The I-IV-V-I Progression

The most common progression in Western music. In C major: I = C, IV = F, V = G. This is in "Let It Be," "Lean on Me," "No Woman No Cry," countless gospel songs.

The vi-IV-I-V Progression

The "pop progression" — used in most modern pop, Afrobeats, and worship songs. In C major: vi = Am, IV = F, I = C, V = G.

NYVC insight: When you know the chord progression, you can predict where the melody is going. Singers who understand harmony sing with the chords — emphasizing chord tones for a grounded, in-tune sound.

Task 3.4

Find a simple gospel hymn. Identify the key and try to figure out the chord progression using I, IV, and V. Write down the progression (e.g., I-IV-I-V-I).

Lesson 3.5: Song Structure — Verse, Chorus, Bridge

Most songs follow a predictable structure. Understanding it helps you learn songs faster, know when to sing with more or less intensity, and even write your own songs.

Common Structures

  • Verse-Chorus-Verse-Chorus-Bridge-Chorus — Most pop, Afrobeats, worship
  • AABA — Classic form, jazz standards, older hymns

What Each Section Does

  • Verse: Tells the story. Lower intensity, conversational.
  • Pre-Chorus: Builds tension toward the chorus.
  • Chorus: The main message, the hook. Highest energy.
  • Bridge: The contrast. Different melody, often the emotional peak.
  • Outro: The wind-down.

Performance insight: Your dynamics should follow the structure. Verse = softer. Chorus = fuller. Bridge = emotional peak. Don't sing everything at the same volume.
