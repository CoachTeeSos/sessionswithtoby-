Lesson 2.1: Resonance — Making Your Voice Carry

Resonance is what makes a voice carry without shouting. It's the difference between someone who sounds good in a small room and someone who fills a cathedral.

The Singer's Formant

Trained singers develop a cluster of overtones around 2800-3400 Hz called the "singer's formant." This is a measurable acoustic phenomenon — it's why a trained voice cuts through a band or orchestra without amplification.

You can't directly control your formant, but you CAN control the space in your vocal tract that creates it:

  • Lower your larynx slightly — like the beginning of a yawn (not a full yawn)
  • Open your pharynx — the back of your throat should feel spacious
  • Lift your soft palate — imagine a small egg in the back of your mouth
  • Keep your tongue forward and relaxed — not bunched up or pulled back

Exercise: Hum an "mmm" and try to feel the vibration in your face (lips, nose, cheekbones). That's forward resonance. Now open to "ah" while keeping that same buzzing feeling. You're learning to place the sound forward.

Forward Placement vs. Back Placement

Singers often talk about "singing from the mask" — this means placing the sound in the front of your face rather than letting it fall back into your throat. Forward placement sounds brighter, clearer, and carries better.

Test: Sing a comfortable note on "ng" (like the end of "sing"). Feel where the vibration is. Now open to "ah" and try to keep that same front-of-face vibration. If the vibration moves back into your throat, you've lost placement.

Lesson 2.2: Vocal Registers — Chest, Mix, Head Voice

Your voice has different "gears" — called registers. Understanding them is essential for singing across your full range without strain.

The Three Main Registers

  • Chest voice (modal): Your speaking voice range. The vocal folds vibrate with their full thickness. Sounds rich and powerful. This is where most pop and gospel singing lives.
  • Head voice: Higher range. The vocal folds stretch and thin out. Sounds lighter, more flute-like. Used in classical, pop falsetto, and high notes.
  • Mix voice: The bridge between chest and head. The most important register to develop — it allows you to sing high notes with power without straining.

Falsetto vs. Head voice: Falsetto is a breathy, disconnected sound where the vocal folds don't fully close. Head voice is connected and resonant. Many singers confuse the two. True head voice has core and clarity; falsetto is airy.

Finding Your Registers

  1. Speak normally — that's chest voice
  2. Slide up from your speaking pitch on "oo" (like "food") — at some point, you'll feel a "flip" — that's the transition to head voice
  3. Now try to keep the same quality going higher — that's mix voice

For worship singers: Most worship singing lives in the mix voice range. Developing a strong mix means you can sing for hours without fatigue. This is the register that sustains long worship sets.

Lesson 2.3: Smoothing the Passaggio (No More Cracks)

The passaggio (Italian for "passage") is the transition point between registers. For most singers, this is where the voice "cracks" or "breaks." Learning to smooth this transition is one of the most important technical skills.

Why Cracks Happen

Cracks happen because the vocal folds are changing from thick vibration (chest) to thin vibration (head). If the change is abrupt, the sound breaks. The goal is to make the change gradual and coordinated.

The NYVC Approach to Smooth Transitions

The NYVC method uses specific exercises to bridge the passaggio:

  • Sirens: Glide from your lowest comfortable note to your highest and back on "ng" or "oo." Don't try to control the sound — just let it slide. This teaches the folds to transition smoothly.
  • Octave slides on vowels: Sing a note, slide up an octave on the same vowel. Start with "oo" (easiest) then try "oh," "ah," "eh," "ee."
  • Descending scales: Start high in head voice and descend into chest voice. Going down through the passaggio is often easier than going up.

Key principle: Don't push through the passaggio. As you approach the transition, lighten your volume slightly and modify your vowel (see Lesson 2.4). Trying to power through with full chest voice is what causes the crack.

Lesson 2.4: Vowel Modification — The Secret to High Notes

As you sing higher, your vowels need to change shape. This is called vowel modification or "covering." It's not about singing differently — it's about adjusting the shape of your mouth and throat to maintain resonance as pitch rises.

The IPA Vowel System for Singers

The International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA) gives us precise names for vowel shapes. Here are the 5 pure vowels used in singing, from most closed to most open:

IPAExampleMouth ShapeUse When...
[i]"ee" in "see"Tongue high and forward, lips spreadHigh head voice, bright tone
[e]"ay" in "day"Tongue mid-high, lips slightly spreadUpper mix, transitioning up
[a]"ah" in "father"Tongue low and flat, mouth openMiddle range, warm tone
[o]"o" in "go"Tongue mid-back, lips roundedLower mix, adding depth
[u]"oo" in "food"Tongue high-back, lips tightly roundedHigh notes, dark tone, power

How Vowel Modification Works

As pitch rises, vowels gradually shift toward more closed shapes:

  • On low notes, you can sing a full open "ah" [a]
  • As you go higher, "ah" naturally becomes more like "aw" [ɔ]
  • Even higher, it becomes more like "oh" [o]
  • At the top of your range, it might be closer to "oo" [u]

You're not changing the word — you're adjusting the vowel shape to maintain resonance. To the listener, it still sounds like the original word. But to your voice, it's a different shape.

NYVC Exercise: Sing "ah" on a 5-tone ascending scale (do-re-mi-fa-sol). On each step up, let the vowel gradually close: ah → aw → oh → oh → oo. Then descend: oo → oh → aw → ah → ah. This trains your voice to modify automatically.

Lesson 2.5: Vibrato — What It Is and How to Control It

Vibrato is the natural oscillation in pitch that gives a voice warmth and life. It's not something you "add" — it's something that happens naturally when the voice is free and well-supported.

The Science of Vibrato

  • Healthy vibrato oscillates at about 5-7 cycles per second (Hz)
  • The pitch variation is typically ±50 cents (half a semitone)
  • It's produced by a reflex in the larynx — not by conscious control
  • Forced vibrato sounds wobbly; natural vibrato sounds effortless

Why Some Singers Don't Have Vibrato

Most commonly, it's caused by tension — in the throat, jaw, or tongue. When the larynx is tight, it can't oscillate freely. The solution is usually:

  • Better breath support (tension often comes from compensating for weak breath)
  • Releasing jaw tension
  • Relaxing the tongue
  • Letting go of the need to control every aspect of the sound

The paradox: Vibrato comes from relaxation, not effort. The more you try to "do" vibrato, the less it happens. Focus on good breath support and a relaxed throat, and vibrato will emerge naturally.

NYVC Vibrato Exercise

  1. Sing a comfortable note and hold it with steady breath
  2. Place your hand gently on your throat
  3. Don't try to create vibrato — just keep the note steady and relaxed
  4. Gently pulse your breath (like a slow "ha-ha-ha" but continuous)
  5. Gradually let the pulsing become smoother and more natural
  6. Eventually, the vibrato should feel like it's happening TO you, not BY you

Lesson 2.6: NYVC Method Exercises — IPA-Based Training

The NYVC (New York Vocal Coaching) method uses the International Phonetic Alphabet as the foundation for all vocal exercises. This ensures precision — when a coach says "sing [i]," every singer produces the exact same vowel shape.

Why IPA Matters for Exercises

Without IPA, saying "sing an ah vowel" is vague. "Ah" in English can be pronounced differently depending on accent, dialect, and context. IPA removes ambiguity:

  • [i] = always the "ee" sound, regardless of language
  • [a] = always the open "ah" sound
  • [u] = always the rounded "oo" sound

This precision means you can practice the exact same exercise as any other singer in the world and get the same result.

The NYVC 5-Vowel Exercise

This is the foundational NYVC exercise. It trains your voice to maintain consistent placement across all vowel shapes:

How to do it:

  1. Choose a comfortable pitch in your middle range
  2. Sing [i] — hold for 4 seconds
  3. Transition to [e] — hold for 4 seconds
  4. Transition to [a] — hold for 4 seconds
  5. Transition to [o] — hold for 4 seconds
  6. Transition to [u] — hold for 4 seconds
  7. Go back down: [u] → [o] → [a] → [e] → [i]

Focus: Keep the volume and tone consistent across all vowels. The only thing that should change is the vowel shape. If [i] is bright and [u] is dark, you're not maintaining consistent placement.

NYVC Ascending Vowel Scale

Once you can hold each vowel, add pitch:

  1. Sing [i] on do, [e] on re, [a] on mi, [o] on fa, [u] on sol
  2. Then reverse: [u] on sol, [o] on fa, [a] on mi, [e] on re, [i] on do
  3. Gradually extend the range upward

Pro tip from NYVC: The [i] and [u] vowels are your best friends for finding head voice placement. If you can sing [i] clearly at a high pitch, you've found head voice. Use [i] as your "map" for the upper register.
