Lesson 2.1: Resonance — Making Your Voice Carry

Resonance is what makes a voice carry without shouting. It's the difference between someone who sounds good in a small room and someone who fills a cathedral.

The Singer's Formant

Trained singers develop a cluster of overtones around 2800-3400 Hz called the "singer's formant." This is a measurable acoustic phenomenon — it's why a trained voice cuts through a band or orchestra without amplification.

You can't directly control your formant, but you CAN control the space in your vocal tract that creates it: lower your larynx slightly, open your pharynx, lift your soft palate, keep your tongue forward and relaxed.

Exercise: Hum an "mmm" and try to feel the vibration in your face (lips, nose, cheekbones). That's forward resonance. Now open to "ah" while keeping that same buzzing feeling. You're learning to place the sound forward.

Task 2.1

Hum "mmm" on a comfortable pitch, then open to "ah" keeping the same front-of-face vibration. Record yourself in BandLab — can you hear the difference between closed and open resonance?

Lesson 2.2: Vocal Registers — Chest, Mix, Head Voice

Your voice has different "gears" — called registers. Understanding them is essential for singing across your full range without strain.

The Three Main Registers

  • Chest voice (modal): Your speaking voice range. The vocal folds vibrate with their full thickness. Sounds rich and powerful.
  • Head voice: Higher range. The vocal folds stretch and thin out. Sounds lighter, more flute-like.
  • Mix voice: The bridge between chest and head. The most important register to develop — it allows you to sing high notes with power without straining.

Falsetto vs. Head voice: Falsetto is a breathy, disconnected sound where the vocal folds don't fully close. Head voice is connected and resonant. True head voice has core and clarity; falsetto is airy.

Task 2.2

Speak normally — that's chest voice. Slide up from your speaking pitch on "oo" — at some point you'll feel a "flip" to head voice. Try to keep the same quality going higher — that's mix voice. Record all three in BandLab.

Lesson 2.3: Smoothing the Passaggio (No More Cracks)

The passaggio (Italian for "passage") is the transition point between registers. This is where the voice "cracks" or "breaks." Learning to smooth this transition is essential.

Why Cracks Happen

Cracks happen because the vocal folds are changing from thick vibration (chest) to thin vibration (head). If the change is abrupt, the sound breaks. The goal is to make the change gradual and coordinated.

NYVC Approach to Smooth Transitions

  • Sirens: Glide from your lowest comfortable note to your highest and back on "ng" or "oo." Don't try to control the sound — just let it slide.
  • Octave slides on vowels: Sing a note, slide up an octave on the same vowel. Start with "oo" (easiest) then try "oh," "ah," "eh," "ee."
  • Descending scales: Start high in head voice and descend into chest voice. Going down through the passaggio is often easier than going up.

Key principle: Don't push through the passaggio. As you approach the transition, lighten your volume slightly and modify your vowel. Trying to power through with full chest voice is what causes the crack.

Task 2.3

Practice sirens: On "ng," glide from your lowest comfortable note to your highest and back down. Do this 5 times. Focus on making the transition as smooth as possible. Record your best attempt in BandLab.

Lesson 2.4: Vowel Modification — The Secret to High Notes

As you sing higher, your vowels need to change shape. This is called vowel modification or "covering." It's not about singing differently — it's about adjusting the shape of your mouth and throat to maintain resonance as pitch rises.

The IPA Vowel System for Singers

IPAExampleMouth ShapeUse When...
[i]"ee" in "see"Tongue high and forward, lips spreadHigh head voice, bright tone
[e]"ay" in "day"Tongue mid-high, lips slightly spreadUpper mix, transitioning up
[a]"ah" in "father"Tongue low and flat, mouth openMiddle range, warm tone
[o]"o" in "go"Tongue mid-back, lips roundedLower mix, adding depth
[u]"oo" in "food"Tongue high-back, lips tightly roundedHigh notes, dark tone, power

How Vowel Modification Works

As pitch rises, vowels gradually shift toward more closed shapes: "ah" → "aw" → "oh" → "oo." You're not changing the word — you're adjusting the vowel shape to maintain resonance.

NYVC Exercise: Sing "ah" on a 5-tone ascending scale. On each step up, let the vowel gradually close: ah → aw → oh → oh → oo. Then descend: oo → oh → aw → ah → ah. This trains your voice to modify automatically.

Task 2.4

Using the IPA vowel chart, sing each pure vowel [i, e, a, o, u] on a single comfortable pitch. Then sing them in sequence on ascending pitches. Record yourself in BandLab.

Lesson 2.5: Vibrato — What It Is and How to Control It

Vibrato is the natural oscillation in pitch that gives a voice warmth and life. It's not something you "add" — it's something that happens naturally when the voice is free and well-supported.

The Science of Vibrato

  • Healthy vibrato oscillates at about 5-7 cycles per second (Hz)
  • The pitch variation is typically ±50 cents (half a semitone)
  • It's produced by a reflex in the larynx — not by conscious control
  • Forced vibrato sounds wobbly; natural vibrato sounds effortless

Why Some Singers Don't Have Vibrato

Most commonly, it's caused by tension — in the throat, jaw, or tongue. When the larynx is tight, it can't oscillate freely.

The paradox: Vibrato comes from relaxation, not effort. The more you try to "do" vibrato, the less it happens. Focus on good breath support and a relaxed throat, and vibrato will emerge naturally.

Task 2.5

Record a sustained note in BandLab (any comfortable pitch, 10+ seconds). Listen back: Do you hear vibrato? Is it natural or forced? If you don't hear it, practice the NYVC exercise for 5 minutes daily for a week, then record again.

Lesson 2.6: Belt Technique — Singing with Power

Belting is singing loudly and powerfully in your upper range without straining. It's not yelling — it's controlled power. This is what separates pop and gospel singers from the rest.

What Belt Is NOT

  • It's NOT pushing more air — that causes strain
  • It's NOT singing in chest voice up high — that causes cracks
  • It's NOT shouting — that damages your voice

What Belt IS

Belting is mix voice with more intensity. The vocal folds are thin and stretched (like head voice) but with more cord closure and more breath support (like chest voice). It's the best of both worlds.

NYVC Belt Exercise

  1. Start with a comfortable "ng" (like the end of "sing") on a mid-range note
  2. Open to "ah" while keeping the same bright, forward placement
  3. Gradually increase volume by adding more breath support — NOT more throat pressure
  4. Slide up by half-steps, maintaining the same bright, powerful quality
  5. Stop the moment you feel strain — belt should feel powerful, not painful

The NYVC rule: If belt hurts, you're doing it wrong. Belt should feel like a powerful shout of joy, not a painful scream. If it hurts, back off and work on your mix voice first.
