Exercise 1

The Hum Slide

Fixes: Disconnected chest and head voice (the "break" in your voice)

Most singers have a noticeable crack or flip when they go from low notes to high notes. This exercise smooths that transition by gently connecting your two vocal registers.

  1. Close your lips gently and hum on "mmm"
  2. Start on your lowest comfortable note
  3. Slowly slide up to your highest comfortable note — like a siren
  4. Slide back down. That's one rep.
What to feel: Vibration moving from your chest (low notes) up to your face/nose (high notes). The transition should feel smooth — no sudden "flip."

💡 Why it works: Humming keeps your vocal folds gently connected. The slow slide trains your voice to transition between registers without the sudden "break" most singers experience.

⭐ Pro tip: If you feel a crack, don't stop. Keep humming through it. The crack is just your voice "switching gears" — with practice, the gears shift smoothly.

Exercise 2

The Lip Trill Scale

Fixes: Throat tension and breath support (the #1 killer of good tone)

If your throat hurts when you sing, you're pushing too hard. The lip trill forces your breath to do the work instead of your throat muscles.

  1. Relax your lips completely — loose and floppy
  2. Push air through them so they vibrate (like a horse sound)
  3. While trilling, sing a 5-note scale: do-re-mi-fa-sol-fa-mi-re-do
  4. Repeat, starting one half-step higher each time
What to feel: Your lips should flutter the entire time. If they stop, you're using too much throat pressure. Your stomach should be working — that's your diaphragm doing its job.

💡 Why it works: Your lips act as a "pressure regulator." They won't vibrate if you're pushing too hard, so your body naturally finds the right balance of air and vocal cord engagement.

⭐ Pro tip: Place your hand on your stomach. It should push out when you breathe in and slowly pull in as you sing. If your stomach isn't moving, you're singing from your throat.

Exercise 3

The "Ng" Resonance Builder

Fixes: Thin, breathy tone (sounds like air is escaping)

A thin tone usually means your voice isn't "placed" forward. This exercise forces resonance into your mask — the area around your nose and cheekbones — which gives you that full, professional sound.

  1. Say the word "sing" — hold the "ng" at the end
  2. Feel where the vibration is: your nose and cheekbones should buzz
  3. Now sing a comfortable note on just "ng" — keep that buzz going
  4. Hold for 5 seconds. Rest. Repeat 5 times.
What to feel: A strong buzz/tingle in your nasal cavity and cheekbones. If you feel it in your throat instead, you're doing it wrong — back off and find the buzz in your face.

💡 Why it works: The "ng" sound naturally directs resonance forward into the "mask." This is the same resonance that makes opera singers sound powerful without a microphone.

⭐ Pro tip: Once you can feel the buzz on "ng," try opening to "ah" while keeping the same placement. That's the secret to a full, resonant tone on any vowel.

Exercise 4

The Siren on "Wee"

Fixes: Limited range and fear of high notes

Most singers don't actually have a range problem — they have a coordination problem. This exercise maps your full range and teaches your voice to glide instead of jump between notes.

  1. Take a relaxed breath
  2. On "wee," slide from your lowest note to your highest — like a fire truck siren
  3. Let it crack, let it break, let it do whatever it wants
  4. Slide back down. That's one rep. Do 5.
What to feel: A continuous glide — no stepping, no jumping. It might feel weird in the middle where your voice "flips." That's normal. Don't try to control it.

💡 Why it works: Sirens bypass your brain's tendency to "grab" at high notes. By gliding instead of stepping, you train your voice to stay relaxed through the entire range.

⭐ Pro tip: Do this exercise every morning for a week. You'll notice your "crack point" moves higher. That's your range expanding in real time.

Exercise 5

The Whisper Sing

Fixes: Singing too hard / straining for volume

Most beginners think singing louder means singing better. It doesn't. This exercise builds proper muscle memory at whisper volume — so when you add volume later, the technique is already correct.

  1. Pick a song you know well
  2. Sing it at full pitch — but whisper volume
  3. Keep your tone placement exactly where it would be if you were singing out loud
  4. Do this for 3 minutes straight
What to feel: Your voice is "singing" but barely making sound. You should feel the resonance in your mask (like Exercise 3) even at whisper volume.

💡 Why it works: When you whisper-sing, you can't rely on volume to "power through" bad technique. Your brain is forced to use the right muscles. When you go back to full volume, those muscles are already trained.

⭐ Pro tip: This is also a great exercise when you're sick or your voice is tired. You're training without straining.

🎵 The Rule

Do all 5 exercises in order. 10 minutes total. Every day for 2 weeks.
Your tone will change. I've seen it happen dozens of times.
