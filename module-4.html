Song Selection — Choose What Serves You

Lesson 4.1: Voice Types and Vocal Weight

Not all voices are built the same. Understanding your voice type helps you choose songs that showcase your strengths instead of fighting your limitations.

Classical Voice Types

  • Bass: E2–E4, heavy, deep
  • Baritone: A2–A4, medium-heavy, warm
  • Tenor: C3–C5, medium-light, bright
  • Alto/Contralto: F3–F5, heavy, rich
  • Mezzo-Soprano: A3–A5, medium, flexible
  • Soprano: C4–C5+, light, soaring

Why this matters: A light lyric soprano singing Adele will strain. A dramatic baritone singing Bruno Mars will struggle. Match the song's weight to your weight.

Task 4.1

Identify your voice type. Sing a song that fits your type and one that doesn't. Record both in BandLab — which sounds better?

Lesson 4.2: Analyzing a Song Before You Sing It

Before you sing a song, analyze it. This saves hours of frustration and helps you make intentional choices.

The 5-Point Song Analysis

  1. Key: What key is it in? Does it fit your tessitura?
  2. Range: Lowest and highest note? Can you hit them comfortably?
  3. Vocal weight: Heavy, medium, or light?
  4. Style: What genre? What's the emotional tone?
  5. Climax: Where's the most intense moment? Is it in your strongest zone?
Task 4.2

Choose 3 songs you'd like to sing. Analyze each using the 5-Point Framework. Write down: Key, Range, Vocal Weight, Style, Climax note, and any red flags.

Lesson 4.3: Transposition — Making Any Song Fit Your Voice

Transposition means changing the key of a song. This is not cheating — it's smart. Every professional singer transposes.

When to Transpose

  • Highest note above your range → transpose down
  • Lowest note below your range → transpose up
  • Song sits in your passaggio → move it to a comfortable zone

The NYVC rule: If you have to strain for more than 2 notes, transpose it. No song is worth damaging your voice.

Task 4.3

Take a simple song. Sing it in its original key, then transpose it up a whole step and sing again. Record both versions in BandLab.

Lesson 4.4: Building a Repertoire That Showcases Your Strengths

Your repertoire is your collection of songs you can sing well. A smart repertoire highlights your strengths and covers different contexts.

The Repertoire Framework

  • Signature song: Sounds like it was written for you
  • Emotional ballad: Slow, intimate, shows dynamics
  • Uptempo song: Fast, rhythmic, shows agility
  • Worship song: For church context
  • Crowd favorite: Well-known, gets people singing
  • Wildcard: Unexpected, shows versatility
Task 4.4

Build your personal repertoire list of 10+ songs. For each, note: the key you'd sing it in, why it fits your voice, and what context it's for.

Lesson 4.5: Context Matters — Worship vs. Stage vs. Studio vs. Social Media

The same song sung in a church, on a stage, in a studio, and for social media requires different approaches.

Worship Context

Goal: Lead people into a spiritual experience. Sing in keys the congregation can follow. Make eye contact with individuals. Keep dynamics controlled.

Stage/Concert Context

Goal: Entertain and connect. Use the mic — pull back on volume. Move on stage. Use dynamics to their fullest.

Social Media Context

Goal: Grab attention in 3 seconds. Start with the hook. Keep it short (15-60 sec). Add captions — most people watch without sound.
