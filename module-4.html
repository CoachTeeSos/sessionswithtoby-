Lesson 4.1: Voice Types and Vocal Weight

Not all voices are built the same. Understanding your voice type helps you choose songs that showcase your strengths instead of fighting your limitations.

The Classical Voice Types

TypeRangeVocal WeightSound Character
BassE2–E4HeavyDeep, rich, grounding
BaritoneA2–A4Medium-HeavyWarm, full, versatile
TenorC3–C5Medium-LightBright, ringing, agile
Alto/ContraltoF3–F5HeavyRich, dark, powerful
Mezzo-SopranoA3–A5MediumWarm, flexible, strong middle
SopranoC4–C5+LightBright, light, soaring

Vocal Weight — Light vs. Heavy

Voice type is about range. Vocal weight is about the natural thickness and power of your voice:

  • Light voices (lyric): Agile, bright, easy in high register. May lack power in low range.
  • Heavy voices (dramatic): Powerful, rich, resonant in low/middle. May struggle with very high notes.
  • Medium voices: The most flexible — can go either direction with training.

Why this matters for song selection: A light lyric soprano singing Adele's "Rolling in the Deep" will strain because that song sits in a heavy, low range. A dramatic baritone singing Bruno Mars' "When I Was Your Man" will struggle with the high notes. Match the song's weight to your weight.

Lesson 4.2: Analyzing a Song Before You Sing It

Before you sing a song, analyze it. This saves you hours of frustration and helps you make intentional choices. Here's a framework:

The 5-Point Song Analysis

  1. Key: What key is it in? Does it fit your tessitura?
  2. Range: What's the lowest and highest note? Can you hit them comfortably?
  3. Vocal weight: Does the song sit in a heavy, medium, or light range?
  4. Style: What genre? What's the emotional tone?
  5. Climax: Where's the most intense moment? Is it in your strongest zone?

Red Flags to Watch For

  • The highest note is at the very top of your range (not sustainable)
  • The song sits mostly in your passaggio (the transition zone — tiring)
  • The emotional climax requires a belt but you're a light voice
  • The song requires sustained notes longer than your breath support allows

Pro tip: If a song has 1-2 red flags, it might still work with transposition or arrangement changes. If it has 3+, choose a different song. There are thousands of songs — don't force one that doesn't fit.

Lesson 4.3: Transposition — Making Any Song Fit Your Voice

Transposition means changing the key of a song. This is not cheating — it's smart. Every professional singer transposes. The original key is not sacred.

When to Transpose

  • The highest note is above your comfortable range → transpose down
  • The lowest note is below your comfortable range → transpose up
  • The song sits in your passaggio → transpose up or down to move it to a more comfortable zone
  • The song doesn't match your vocal weight → transpose to a key that suits your voice

How to Transpose

  1. Identify the original key (use a piano app or search "[song name] key")
  2. Find your ideal key based on your tessitura (from Module 1)
  3. Calculate the interval difference (e.g., moving from C to D is up a Major 2nd)
  4. Use a backing track in the new key, or play chords yourself
  5. Sing and adjust — sometimes a half-step makes all the difference

The NYVC rule: If you have to strain for more than 2 notes in a song, transpose it. No song is worth damaging your voice. And no audience has ever complained that a song was in a different key — they only remember how it made them feel.

Lesson 4.4: Building a Repertoire That Showcases Your Strengths

Your repertoire is your collection of songs you can sing well. A smart repertoire highlights your strengths and covers different contexts.

The Repertoire Framework

Aim for at least one song in each category:

  • Your signature song: The one that sounds like it was written for you. Showcases your unique voice.
  • The emotional ballad: Slow, intimate, shows your dynamics and control.
  • The uptempo/energy song: Fast, rhythmic, shows your agility and groove.
  • The worship/spiritual song: For church context. Shows your spiritual depth.
  • The crowd favorite: A well-known song that gets people singing along.
  • The wildcard: Something unexpected that shows range and versatility.

Context Matters

Different situations demand different songs:

  • Worship leading: Songs the congregation knows, in singable keys, with clear melodies
  • Concert/stage: Songs that show range, dynamics, and emotional depth
  • Social media: Short, catchy, visually engaging (think 30-60 second clips)
  • Wedding/event: Romantic, appropriate, well-known
  • Audition: Songs that showcase your unique voice, not just technical ability

Coach Toby's advice: Don't build a repertoire of songs you "can sing." Build a repertoire of songs you love to sing. Passion is audible. If you're not emotionally connected to a song, the audience won't be either.

Lesson 4.5: Context Matters — Worship vs. Stage vs. Studio vs. Social Media

The same song sung in a church, on a stage, in a studio, and for social media requires different approaches. Understanding context makes you a versatile singer.

Worship Context

  • Goal: Lead people into a spiritual experience, not perform
  • Key consideration: Sing in keys the congregation can follow (not too high)
  • Style: Clear diction, emotional sincerity, space for the Spirit to move
  • Common mistake: Over-singing. Worship is about facilitating, not impressing.

Stage/Concert Context

  • Goal: Entertain, move, and connect with an audience
  • Key consideration: Choose keys that let you perform with energy for the full set
  • Style: Dynamics, stage presence, emotional peaks and valleys
  • Common mistake: Singing everything at full voice. Contrast is what makes a performance memorable.

Studio Context

  • Goal: Capture a perfect (or emotionally perfect) take
  • Key consideration: Studio singing is more intimate — every detail is heard
  • Style: Nuance over power. Subtlety over spectacle.
  • Common mistake: Performing for the mic like it's a stage. Studio singing is a conversation, not a proclamation.

Social Media Context

  • Goal: Grab attention in the first 3 seconds and hold it
  • Key consideration: Short clips (15-60 seconds), visual engagement, trending sounds
  • Style: Hook-first approach. Start with the most impactful part, not the intro.
  • Common mistake: Posting full songs. Social media rewards the hook, not the whole journey.
